"It's the little things citizens do. That's what will make the difference.
My little thing is planting trees."

In 1977, a Kenyan academic named Wangari Maathai walked into her backyard in Nairobi with seven seedlings and a belief that one person could matter. She planted each tree with her own hands on a quiet afternoon — no cameras, no applause, no ceremony. What that single act of quiet defiance grew into became one of the most extraordinary environmental movements in human history.

The Green Belt Movement, born from those seven trees, would go on to plant over 51 million trees across Africa, restore devastated watersheds, and transform the lives of tens of thousands of rural women. In 2004, Wangari Maathai became the first African woman to receive the Nobel Peace Prize. But she never stopped talking about those first seven trees.

Her story is not about exceptional talent or extraordinary resources. It is about something far more powerful: the willingness to begin. That is the spirit behind Young Humanitarians in Action. We do not wait for government. We do not wait for permission. We begin — right here, in the streets of our communities, with our hands and our hearts and whatever tools we have.

— Inspired by Wangari Maathai, Nobel Peace Laureate & Kenyan Environmental Pioneer
The Crisis at Our Doorstep

When Dirty Streets Become a Death Sentence

Environmental neglect is not an abstract concept. It is a flood that swallows a child while she sleeps. It is a blocked drain that turns a Nairobi highway into a river. It is the direct and measurable result of years of untreated waste, carelessly discarded plastic, and a city allowed to choke on its own litter.

April – May 2024

The Floods That Nairobi Could Have Prevented

In the long rains of April and May 2024, Kenya experienced one of its most devastating flood seasons in living memory. The catastrophe unfolded with a cruel logic: heavy rainfall struck a city whose drainage systems — many designed decades ago for a far smaller population — had never been properly maintained, and whose channels were choked with plastic bags, polythene, discarded food packaging, and every form of urban litter imaginable.

Water with nowhere to go became a weapon. The Nairobi River and the Athi River burst their banks. Informal settlements — Kibera, Mathare, Mukuru — where infrastructure is poorest and residents are most exposed, bore the worst of it. In Mathare, residents had been warning for years that blocked drains would one day cause a catastrophe. Nobody listened. Then came April 2024.

Residents of South C and Grogan watched helplessly as water poured into their homes through clogged drainage systems. In Kirinyaga Road, the Nairobi River burst without warning on a Friday evening, sweeping away cars from mechanical workshops, dragging engines, tools, and livelihoods into a churning brown current. "I just watched as the vehicles were swept away," said Paul Oduor, a garage owner in Grogan. "Some were submerged, others rolled into the river."

Urban planners and residents had long warned that Nairobi's drainage system — much of it designed decades ago for a much smaller city — had not kept pace with the capital's explosive growth. The problem is compounded by blocked drains filled with plastic waste, illegal construction along waterways, and years of poor maintenance. A decade earlier, in 2015, the same thing had happened. And the decade before that. "This is the third time this has happened here," said one resident. "Every time, we cry, we complain and then everything goes back to normal."

March 2026 — It Happened Again

Nairobi's Drainage Crisis Is Not a Natural Disaster

Two years after the catastrophic floods of 2024, Nairobi flooded again. In early March 2026, overnight rainfall caused rivers across the capital to overflow. Uhuru Highway turned into a canal. Mombasa Road was impassable. Vehicles were swept from streets in Grogan. The Nairobi Expressway opened its lanes for free just so commuters could find a road above water.

The death toll from those March 2026 floods rose to at least 66 across the country. In Nairobi alone, approximately 3,500 households were affected. Residents of Mukuru, Kibera, Mathare, Pipeline, Githurai, South B, South C, Roysambu, and Westlands woke to water in their homes, their streets, their lives.

The cause? Exactly what it was in 2024. Exactly what it was in 2015. Blocked drainage systems. Uncollected garbage. Decades of plastic waste silently accumulating in rivers and storm drains, turning Nairobi's veins into walls. City Hall eventually dispatched 4,000 Green Army personnel to unclog drains — an emergency response to a crisis that should never have been allowed to exist.

Greenpeace Africa described the floods as "not a surprise." Climate change, driven by human pollution, is intensifying Kenya's rainfall patterns. A warmer atmosphere holds more moisture. Storms are becoming more destructive. And Nairobi — with its uncollected waste, its overflowing bins, its rivers clogged with polythene — remains catastrophically unprepared. This is the world in which YHA was born. And this is the world we are determined to change.

The Root Cause

Pollution Is the Hidden Author of These Tragedies

Every polythene bag thrown from a matatu window. Every bottle tossed into the Nairobi River. Every pile of rubbish left to rot at an informal settlement that a garbage truck never visits. These are not small acts of carelessness. They are, in aggregate, acts of collective destruction that end in death.

Nairobi's informal settlements — home to more than half the city's population — sit at the lowest point of a chain of neglect. Residents have the least political voice, the fewest municipal services, and the most exposure to the consequences of environmental decay. When the drains flood, they flood first. When cholera spreads (and the Kenya Ministry of Health confirmed a fourth cholera outbreak following the 2024 floods), it spreads fastest among communities where waste water and drinking water share the same space.

Researchers from the Africa Research and Impact Network have documented what residents of Kibera and Mathare have known for generations: the crisis is not weather. The crisis is a structural failure sustained by poverty, neglect, and a cultural acceptance of environmental degradation. Changing that culture — one neighbourhood, one cleaned street, one young person at a time — is exactly what Young Humanitarians in Action exists to do.

Here Is Where We Come In

We Don't Wait for Someone Else to Fix It

Young Humanitarians in Action is a grassroots organisation born in the heart of Nairobi's most vulnerable communities. We are young people — volunteers, dreamers, and doers — who have decided that the gap between the world as it is and the world as it should be is a gap worth crossing.

We pick up litter that clogs the drains. We organise cleaning days in slums that county governments ignore. We knock on the doors of children's homes carrying food, clothing, and school supplies. We walk into communities with no proper sanitation and ask: what do you need, and how can we help?

We are not a corporation. We are not funded by international NGOs chasing donor metrics. We are young Kenyans who grew up watching the suffering in our communities and decided — enough. We cannot wait for the perfect government, the perfect policy, or the perfect plan. We work with what we have, where we are.

Because every cleaned drain is one less family whose home floods. Every bag of food delivered to a children's home is one less child who goes to bed hungry. Every child we teach about environmental stewardship is one less person who will grow up throwing waste into a river. Change is cumulative. And it starts today.

What We Do

Three Pillars of a Better Nairobi

Every action YHA takes falls under one of three core commitments — all of them grounded in the conviction that a cleaner, kinder world is not a dream. It is a choice made in action, day after day.

Environmental Service

We organise community clean-ups in Nairobi's slums and underserved settlements — clearing blocked drains, collecting garbage, and cleaning the streets that formal waste management forgets. We believe a clean environment is not a luxury. It is a right. And we act on that belief every time we show up with gloves and refuse sacks.

Read more

Charity & Outreach

We donate food, clothing, school supplies, and essential goods to children's homes across Nairobi, and to families in need within the communities we serve. No child should face the day hungry or ill-equipped to learn. Our outreach teams build relationships — not just deliveries — because real charity is built on dignity.

Read more

Community Education

We visit schools and community centres in the slums to teach children and young adults about environmental conservation, waste management, and why keeping their surroundings clean is not just helpful — it is life-saving. An informed community is the most powerful environmental tool there is.
